# 45-Minute Literature Presentation Outline

Use this as a flexible starting point. A typical main deck contains 38–46 slides, with 8–18 appendix slides. Depth is measured by the quality of explanation, not page count.

## A. Opening and Article Profile — 3 minutes, 3–4 slides

1. Title slide.
2. Journal profile: cover, IF/year, JCR, CAS partition/version, publication date, DOI.
3. Authors, affiliations, country/region, funding, registration.
4. Optional: why the paper was selected.

## B. Abstract and Roadmap — 2 minutes, 1–2 slides

5. Structured abstract.
6. Roadmap, when needed.

## C. Introduction and Theoretical Foundation — 9–12 minutes, usually 7–11 slides

Possible sequence:

7. Burden and why the issue matters.
8. Target population and developmental/clinical context.
9. Core concept and evidence boundary.
10–12. Theory or framework, using only the pages needed to explain the paper.
13. Current evidence.
14. Limitation of dominant approaches.
15. Specific scientific and methodological gap.
16. Study response, conceptual model, aims, or hypotheses.

The section must form one logical line. Do not split theory merely to reach a quota.

## D. Methods — 10–12 minutes, usually 8–12 slides

17. Design, setting, and rationale.
18. Participants and recruitment.
19. Inclusion/exclusion criteria and sample.
20. Data-collection timeline and quality control.
21–23. Measures and variable roles.
24–27. Statistical analysis: question → input → preprocessing → model → parameters → diagnostics → output.
28. Integrated analysis workflow.

## E. Results — 12–15 minutes, usually 11–15 slides

29. Participant flow or sample profile.
30. Descriptive findings.
31–40. Primary and secondary results, one major conclusion per slide.
41. Robustness, stability, sensitivity, or subgroup analysis.
42. Integrated answer to the research questions.

The original figure/table or a native redraw should be readable without zooming.

## F. Discussion and Methodological Appraisal — 7–9 minutes, usually 6–9 slides

43. Findings mapped to research questions.
44–46. Comparison with prior evidence and mechanisms.
47. Clinical/theoretical implications.
48. Strengths and limitations.
49. Independent methodological appraisal.
50. Key questions and improvements.

## G. Summary — 2 minutes, 1–2 slides

51. What the study added.
52. What should be interpreted cautiously.

## H. Implications for the User's Research — 3–5 minutes, 3–5 slides

53. Transferable research question/theory.
54. Population, timing, instruments, variables, and covariates.
55. Statistical adaptation and robustness.
56. Writing/visualization lessons and what not to copy.
57. Potential paper or extension.

## I. Appendix — 8–18 slides

- evidence-search map;
- full theory map;
- scale scoring;
- extended methods;
- code and parameter glossary;
- additional tables/figures;
- stability/sensitivity results;
- evidence tracker and unresolved issues.

## Divider Rule

Use no more than four section-divider slides in the main deck unless the user approves more.

## Visual Rule

Do not use a fixed occupancy percentage. Preserve intentional negative space, avoid decorative filling, and use the approved page archetypes and visual benchmark.

## Mandatory Pre-Production Rule

Before image generation or PPT construction, list every planned slide with its title, scientific question, main takeaway, previous/current/next logic, exact content, evidence, visual form, image2 prompt summary, reconstruction plan, and references. Obtain user approval of this full-deck blueprint.

## Mandatory Image2 Rule

After approval, generate a full-slide image2/image_gen reference for every page. Then reconstruct each page element by element in editable PowerPoint. Do not use the full-slide generated image as a background.

## Result-Slide Rule

Use one figure, one coherent figure panel, one table, or one analytical conclusion per slide, with one adjacent interpretation. Split multi-panel figures when panels answer different questions.
