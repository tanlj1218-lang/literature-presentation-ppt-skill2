# 45-Minute Literature Presentation Outline

Use this as a detailed starting point rather than a rigid page count. The main deck will usually contain 45–55 slides, with 8–18 appendix slides.

## A. Opening and Article Profile — 3 minutes, 3–4 slides

1. Title slide.
2. Journal profile: cover, IF/year, JCR, CAS partition/version, publication date, DOI.
3. Authors, affiliations, country/region, funding, registration.
4. Optional: why this paper was selected and its place in the field.

## B. Abstract and Roadmap — 2 minutes, 2 slides

5. Structured abstract: background, objective, methods, results, conclusion.
6. Navigation/roadmap.

## C. Introduction and Theoretical Foundation — 9–12 minutes, 8–12 slides

7. Clinical/scientific burden and why the issue matters.
8. Target population and developmental/clinical context.
9. Core concepts and precise definitions.
10. Original theoretical framework: origin and propositions.
11. Core constructs and mechanism/pathway.
12. Mapping theory to the paper's variables and measures.
13. Mapping theory to the analytical method.
14. Current evidence and consistent findings.
15. Conflicting evidence and unresolved questions.
16. Limitations of dominant designs or methods.
17. Specific scientific gap and study entry point.
18. Aim, research questions, hypotheses, and conceptual model.

Every slide in this section must contain references. Theory slides should cite the original theory plus later application/review/critique sources.

## D. Methods — 10–12 minutes, 10–14 slides

19. Study design, setting, and design rationale.
20. Participants and recruitment.
21. Inclusion and exclusion criteria with rationale.
22. Sample-size rationale and final sample.
23. Data-collection timeline.
24. Data quality control and missing-data handling.
25–28. Instruments/measures: construct, structure, scoring, reliability/validity, timing.
29. Variables, roles, and covariates.
30–33. Statistical analysis: question → input → preprocessing → model → parameters → diagnostics → output.
34. Analysis workflow summary.

Every method slide must cite the target paper and, when explaining methods beyond the paper, the original methods paper or official documentation.

## E. Results — 12–15 minutes, 12–17 slides

35. Participant flow.
36. Sample characteristics.
37. Variable distributions or measurement characteristics.
38–49. Primary and secondary results, one major conclusion per slide.
50. Robustness, sensitivity, stability, or subgroup result.
51. Integrated answer to the research questions.

Result slides must cite the target paper and show the original figure/table number when relevant.

## F. Discussion and Methodological Appraisal — 7–9 minutes, 7–10 slides

52. Main findings mapped to research questions.
53. Finding 1 compared with prior evidence.
54. Finding 2 compared with prior evidence.
55. Mechanism or reason 1.
56. Mechanism or reason 2.
57. Clinical and theoretical implications.
58. Strengths and author-reported limitations.
59. Independent methodological appraisal.
60. Three to five substantive questions and improvements.
61. External validity and translation to practice.

Every discussion slide must contain references. Statements of agreement, disagreement, and mechanism must be linked to named studies or reviews.

## G. Summary — 2 minutes, 1–2 slides

62. What the study added.
63. What should be interpreted cautiously.

## H. Implications for the User's Research — 3–5 minutes, 4–6 slides

64. Transferable research question and theoretical framework.
65. Population, time points, instruments, variables, and covariates.
66. Statistical methods and parameter adaptation.
67. Writing and visualization lessons.
68. What must not be copied directly.
69. Possible standalone paper or methodological extension.

## I. Appendix — 8–18 slides

- literature search and evidence map;
- full theory map;
- data structure;
- scale scoring details;
- extended methods;
- complete code;
- parameter glossary;
- additional tables/figures;
- stability and sensitivity analysis;
- evidence tracker summary;
- unresolved issues.

## Slide-Balance Rule

An ordinary slide should use approximately 70–88% of the main content area. If it uses less than about 60%, enrich or merge it. If it exceeds about 90%, split it. Title and section-divider slides are exceptions.
