# Evidence Search, Theory Reconstruction, and Citation Policy

## 1. Purpose

This guide ensures that the introduction, theoretical foundation, methods, discussion, appraisal, and implications are supported by an adequate and traceable evidence base rather than unsupported presenter opinion.

## 2. Minimum Evidence Architecture

For a typical 45-minute report, build an external evidence set of approximately 12–20 high-quality sources in addition to the target paper. Complex theoretical or methodological papers may require more. The evidence set should normally include:

- 1–2 original or seminal theory/framework sources;
- 2–4 recent systematic reviews, meta-analyses, or authoritative reviews;
- 3–6 directly relevant empirical studies;
- 1–3 original methods papers or official software/package documents;
- official scale manuals or development/validation papers;
- authoritative burden, guideline, or policy sources when relevant.

Do not add sources merely to reach a quota. Every source must support a specific slide claim.

## 3. Source Priority

Prefer, in order:

1. original theory or methods papers;
2. official manuals, guidelines, package documentation, and institutional sources;
3. systematic reviews and meta-analyses;
4. high-quality longitudinal, experimental, or large-sample empirical studies;
5. relevant cross-sectional studies;
6. narrative reviews only when primary or systematic evidence is unavailable.

Avoid relying on secondary summaries for precise parameter definitions or theory claims when the original source can be located.

## 4. Search Documentation

Record:

- database or website;
- search date;
- search terms;
- date range;
- source type;
- article title and DOI;
- exact claim supported;
- slide number(s);
- whether full text, abstract, or official documentation was checked.

Use `templates/literature-evidence-matrix.csv`.

## 5. Introduction Evidence Chain

The introduction should normally move through these evidence layers:

1. **Burden** — prevalence, incidence, disease burden, functional consequences, or service need.
2. **Population context** — developmental, clinical, cultural, or care-setting characteristics.
3. **Core concepts** — precise definitions and boundaries.
4. **Theory** — the model that explains why the variables should be related.
5. **Current evidence** — what prior studies consistently show.
6. **Inconsistency** — where results differ and why.
7. **Methodological limitation** — what current designs or analyses cannot resolve.
8. **Scientific gap** — the exact unanswered question.
9. **Study response** — why the present design and method are appropriate.
10. **Aim/hypothesis** — the direct question tested.

Every layer must be supported by references. Avoid vague phrases such as “research is limited” unless the reviewed evidence is shown.

## 6. Theoretical Foundation Module

A theory section should answer six questions:

1. Who proposed the theory and what problem was it designed to explain?
2. What are its core constructs?
3. What causal or dynamic relationships does it propose?
4. How do those constructs map to this paper's variables, measures, and design?
5. Why does the selected analytical method fit the theory?
6. What are the theory's limitations, critiques, or competing explanations?

Recommended slides:

- Slide A: origin, purpose, and core propositions;
- Slide B: constructs and pathways;
- Slide C: mapping to the paper;
- Slide D: theory-to-method alignment;
- Slide E: limitations and complementary theories;
- Slide F: integrated conceptual model.

Each theory slide should cite the original theory source and at least one later application, review, or critique.

## 7. Slide-Level Citation Rules

### Mandatory references

References are mandatory on:

- article profile slides;
- introduction and burden slides;
- concept-definition slides;
- theory slides;
- measurement and instrument slides;
- statistical-method slides;
- results slides;
- discussion and mechanism slides;
- methodological appraisal slides;
- implications-for-user-research slides.

Only title, section-divider, and roadmap slides are normally exempt.

### Typical citation density

- one simple factual claim: 1 source;
- multiple background claims: 2–4 sources;
- theory slide: original theory + 1–3 later sources;
- method slide: original methods paper and/or official documentation;
- result slide: target paper, figure/table number, and external source only when interpreting beyond the article;
- discussion/mechanism slide: target paper + 2–4 comparison or mechanism sources;
- appraisal slide: reporting guideline or methods source where relevant.

### Footer format

Use concise references that remain readable, for example:

`Borsboom & Cramer, Annu Rev Clin Psychol, 2013; Lunansky et al., Methods, 2022.`

The full reference list should appear in the appendix or delivery package.

## 8. Theory and Evidence QA

Before finalizing, verify:

- every major claim has a matching source;
- the cited source actually supports the claim;
- original theory and methods sources were consulted;
- recent reviews were used to update the field;
- conflicting evidence is not hidden;
- the theory is mapped explicitly to the study;
- the analytical method is not presented as theoretically justified unless that link is explained;
- reference text is readable in the rendered deck.
